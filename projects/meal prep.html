MEAL.PREP

Food delivery app

Meal.Prep is an app that allows you to create a complete meal plan for a variable number of days (up to a month). You can apply various filters based on your preferences—such as gluten-free, fish-only, and so on.

Challenge

The primary goal was to build an application that was intuitive and easy to use. Users should not have to spend a lot of time in the app; the objective is simply to place an order. That is precisely why it was essential to keep everything simple and straightforward.

My role

  • App design from initial sketches to final design in three weeks.
  • Creating a design system from scratch.
  • Handing off designs to developers, including explanations and necessary components.
  • Creating a clickable prototype to identify weak points in the application.

User

A person aged 20 to 40; gender does not matter. The user is a busy individual who enjoys planning, values ​​their time, and cares about their health. Eating a healthy diet is important to them.

Process

The project went through a full design cycle — from market research and moodboard to final UI and testing. Three phases, each with its own focus.

Phase 3

Concept Exploration

3 Concepts Visual language Direction choice

Since this application was being developed for an established company that already had its own brand identity, I used their brand color for the call-to-action (CTA) elements. However, the app's visual style could be created from scratch, without being constrained by the parent website's design.

Three stylistic concepts were developed, and a flat design approach was ultimately chosen because it is simple and does not place an unnecessary cognitive load on the user.

Phase 2

Design System

Tokens Components Dev docs Built from scratch

A design system is the foundation of any design, as it ensures consistency and helps avoid errors. I always create design systems for the projects I work on, even for single-page sites. This benefits both me and the developer, as they have all the information needed to build the site. In my designs, I aim to follow an 8-pixel grid.

The design system for this project wasn't large or complex, but it really streamlined the work on the application, allowing me to complete the design quite quickly.

Some UX decisions

Decision 1: Create not just a chat, but a chat with frequently asked questions.

The initial design did not include an FAQ section; there was simply a support chat. However, after some consideration, we decided to add one. A minor challenge arose: we needed not only to add the section but also to ensure users could return to it after opening the chat page. We also wanted to avoid fixing it in place, as is often done with website chat widgets. Consequently, we separated the two screens, keeping the chat and the FAQ page distinct.

Decision 2: Guest modes

To engage the user immediately, it was decided to allow them to proceed through several screens as a guest before requiring registration. This will clearly increase the number of users who complete the process. By the time they are asked to register or log in, the user will have already selected their dietary preferences.

Decision 3: Collapsible parts

Since this is a mobile app, we had to account for limited screen real estate, especially given the use of fixed buttons and menus—not to mention the device's status bar and home indicator. Consequently, we decided to make certain elements collapsible to save space. For instance, we applied this approach to the order details on the payment page; while the information is significant overall, it is less critical at that specific stage, so we collapsed the section. This freed up space for more important information regarding payment methods.
