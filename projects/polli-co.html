Polli.co

AI-powered Web3 voting platform

I designed a Web3 platform that helps users delegate voting power with AI-assisted recommendations. The product included a dashboard, delegation flow, marketing website, brand identity, and a scalable design system.

Challenge

Web3 voting and delegation are complex for non-technical users. The main challenge was to make the delegation process clear, transparent, and trustworthy, especially when AI recommendations were involved.

User

The target audience is technically literate users aged 22–50 who understand the crypto market well but want to spend less time on operational decisions. Transparency, control, and speed are what they care about.

Process

The project went through a full design cycle — from market research and moodboard to final UI and testing. Three phases, each with its own focus.

How the work unfolded

Each phase built on the previous one. Here are the key moments — not everything, just what genuinely shaped the final result.

Phase 1

Moodboard

Visual direction Tone & feel

The crypto space is saturated with aggressive aesthetics — dark backgrounds, neon colours, a sense of risk. We wanted something different: a feeling of control, precision, and trust. The moodboard helped align this with the tone before any real design work began.

The core challenge was explaining a complex financial process through a simple interface. We tested with real users — people who already work with token delegation. Key UX decisions

Decision 1: Reduced delegation from 7 steps to 3

Early flows were too long and created unnecessary cognitive load. I simplified the process into three key actions: choose delegation type, review AI recommendation, confirm delegation.

Decision 2: Streamlining withdrawals

In some networks, it was possible to withdraw funds from multiple validators at once. This required designing a modal window that supported multiple selections, while ensuring the user wasn't overwhelmed with information.

It was also decided to retain the option to withdraw from a single validator, including the ability to specify a token amount (an option that is technically unavailable for multiple validators). Thus, selecting a single validator would bring up an input field.

Decision 3: Streamlining withdrawals

At the very first stage of the login process, the user needed to connect a wallet. However, we needed to indicate which wallets were installed in the browser but not yet connected, which ones were not installed at all, and which ones were already connected. To address this, we defined three wallet statuses. We also devised a color scheme for each status so that the user could intuitively understand what each one signified.

Where the project landed

The product reached final UI stage and was handed off to development. In 2026 the project was paused due to the crypto market downturn — a decision unrelated to the quality of the design or the product.
